 - **Write a tutorial on the polar representation and hyperbolic distance at large radius.** It should make two points, honestly. (1) *The win:* the polar chart `(r, v)` computes geodesic distances correctly where the ambient charts fail — same-angle points with `Δr = 1` give distance `1.0` at every radius via `manifolds.polar.polar_distances` (stable law of cosines, `cosh d − 1` as a sum of non-negatives + `log1p`), whereas `geoopt.Lorentz.dist` returns `−0.693 / NaN / −∞` past `r ≈ 18` (Minkowski inner-product cancellation → guarded `arccosh`) and the Poincaré ball collapses every `r ≳ 12` to `12.20` (`tanh(r/2) → 1`). (2) *The honest caveat / the limit discovered building Stage A:* near-coincident **directions** at large radius are fundamentally float64-unresolvable in *any* chart — the angular term `~ sinh²(r)·sin²(Δθ/2)` must stand above the `~ε·sinh²(r)` noise from `v` not being exactly unit-norm, so `Δθ` below `~√ε` (and, at large `r`, more) is lost. Source material already in place: `hypegrl/manifolds/polar.py`, `tests/test_charts.py` (mpmath oracle + failure-pairs guard), the standalone `reproduce_dmercator_nan.py` / `native_distance.py`, the `dmercator_radial_collapse.tex` writeup (all in the repo parent), and — the richest source — `experiments/representation_comparison.py` + `experiments/results/representation_conditioning.png` (the Stage-D comparison). The tutorial should build on the three ambient-chart failure modes it surfaces, all worth showing honestly: (i) **ball radial saturation** — wrong-but-finite for every large-r pair; (ii) **hyperboloid Minkowski cancellation** — NaN/negative distances for near-coincident pairs past r≈18; (iii) **hyperboloid radial run-away** — even where distances are fine, the e^r-scale coords are unstable under RiemannianAdam and blow the radius out to the max_norm ceiling (r≈42), so the *refinement itself* diverges. Plus the downstream payoff: OpenFlights reconstruction AUC polar 0.99 vs ball 0.51 (≈random) vs hyperboloid FAIL. Good candidate for `docs/` following the nbsphinx bake-outputs workflow.
 - All optimization should be performed in the Hyperboloid model by default.
 - **Build an "exact polar H^{D+1}" `geoopt.Manifold` and evaluate whether it's worth the effort.** The current `PolarRepresentation` optimises `v` on `geoopt.Sphere` and `r = softplus(u)` on `geoopt.Euclidean`, i.e. under the *product* metric `du² ⊕ dΩ²` — **not** the true warped hyperbolic metric `ds² = dr² + sinh²(r)·dΩ²`. So its RiemannianAdam step is **not** the Riemannian gradient of H^{D+1}: (1) the angular gradient misses the `1/sinh²(r)` factor, and (2) — the deeper issue — the update is a product retraction (sphere-expmap ⊕ Euclidean step), so the movement is *not* along the hyperbolic geodesic (exact only for pure-radial moves, where radial lines through the origin *are* geodesics; angular moves step along a great circle of `S^D` = an equidistant hyperbolic circle, off-geodesic, most at large `r`). This is *accepted for now*: fixed points are metric-independent, so it descends the same loss to the same minima, and Stage D shows it beats the ambient charts on real large-r graphs. The exact fix: a custom manifold whose `egrad2rgrad` inserts the `1/sinh²(r)` warp and whose `expmap`/`retr` is the coupled `(r, v)` hyperbolic geodesic — then polar coincides *geometrically* with the ball/hyperboloid (genuine Riemannian) while keeping the polar chart's numerical conditioning. A/B it against the product retraction in `experiments/representation_comparison.py` (Tier 3) to decide if the extra code earns its keep. Full analysis in `REPRESENTATION_PLAN.md` → Stage E design note. (We keep the exact hyperboloid/ball charts regardless — they are truly Riemannian but carry their own failures: ball saturation r≈12, hyperboloid cancellation r≈18 + radial run-away.)
 - **Add a loss-layer "distance scale / temperature" `τ` (= √curvature) to make curvature a first-class, uniform knob.** Curvature is a global *metric scale*, not a chart property: H of curvature −c is isometric to the c=1 space under `d ↦ d·√c`, so scaling the pairwise distances *inside the loss* (`d ↦ d/τ`) **is** changing the curvature. Today this is scattered and implicit, and each method hides it differently:
   - **Hydra+** folds `√k` into its target distances (`s_A = D·√k`, optimised on the c=1 chart, ≡ embedding at curvature k).
   - **Fermi-Dirac** already contains exactly this τ, under another name: in HyperMap/E-PSO `ζ = √(−K) = √k`, and since `R̂` is itself a distance, the exponent `(ζ/2T)(d_k − R̂_k)` **is** `(1/2T)(d₁ − R̂₁)` — i.e. ζ *is* the rescaling of distances to the unit chart, not a separate knob absorbing curvature into it, and `R̂` absorbs nothing (it merely has to be in the same units). The consequence worth naming: only the product `ζ/2T` ever reaches the likelihood (carried as the single scalar `zeta_over_2T`), so **curvature and temperature are non-identifiable** — a Fermi-Dirac fit determines their ratio and never `k` and `T` separately. Adopting τ here is therefore a *renaming and unification* of ζ, after which `β = 1/2T` is purely an inverse temperature instead of doing double duty as the metric scale.
   - The **Poincaré/Lorentz ranking losses have no explicit temperature at all**.

   Unify it: a single scalar `τ` applied to `rep.dist()` in the loss closure, optionally a **learnable** torch parameter optimised jointly (like `a_Ω`) so a method can fit curvature by gradient rather than Hydra's closed-form pre-fit. This deliberately lives in the loss/inference layer, **not** the `Representation` (which stays curvature-free by design: curvature is a global metric scale, absorbable into the loss, so it is not a chart property). Would give the ranking methods a curvature/temperature they currently lack, and make "fitted curvature" a uniform capability instead of a Hydra+-only trick.

   **Measured stakes — this is worth real F1, not just tidiness.** On Hydra+, where the knob is already exposed (`curvature=None` → 1-D stress-minimising search), enabling it is the *entire* improvement over the v1 paper's numbers: on link prediction (5 seeds, q=0.9) ToggleSwitch 49.98 → **64.43** and MyeloidProgenitors 46.28 → **71.38** versus the same fit at the fixed `k=1` both the Hydra paper (§4.1: "hyperbolic curvature was fixed to −κ = −1") and v1 used. The fitted optimum is `k ≈ 0.096` / `0.066` — an order of magnitude from 1 — and swapping the optimiser at matched `k` moves F1 by only ≈ −2. Olsson gains nothing (10.8 → 11.0), so it is graph-dependent, not universal.

   **Next experiment: does a fitted distance scale pay off for the other methods too?** Most of it needs **no refactor** — 4 of the 6 gradient methods already carry a τ under another name, so a sweep is possible today:
   - `HydraPlusEmbedder(curvature=…)` — done, above.
   - `PoincareMapsEmbedder(gamma=…)` — the decoder is `softmax(−d/γ)`, so γ *is* τ exactly.
   - `HyperMapEmbedder` / `DMercatorEmbedder` — the Fermi-Dirac exponent `zeta_over_2T·(R̂ − d)` scales as a whole when distances are rescaled (R̂ being a distance too), so `zeta_over_2T` is the τ knob; settle the double-application question below before trusting a ζ ≠ 1 sweep.
   - `PoincareEmbeddingsEmbedder` / `LorentzEmbeddingsEmbedder` — ranking losses with **no** scale parameter; these are the two that genuinely require the τ to be built, and are therefore the real test of whether it is worth adding.

   Suggested order: (1) sweep the existing knobs on the Table I graphs to see whether the Hydra+ effect generalises; (2) only if it does, build the learnable τ and check that fitting it by gradient recovers the swept optimum; (3) then extend to the two ranking methods. Worth reporting even if negative — a null result would show Hydra+'s sensitivity is peculiar to a *stress* objective (which pins absolute distances) rather than to ranking/likelihood objectives (which are far more scale-tolerant); that hypothesis is ours, untested.

   **Open question to settle first (unverified):** `fermi_dirac_nll_from_dist` computes `sigmoid(zeta_over_2T * (R_pair − D))` with `D = rep.dist()`, which is a **unit-curvature** distance (every `Representation` fixes k=1). By the identity above the unit-chart exponent is `(1/2T)(d₁ − R̂₁)` with *no* ζ, so for `ζ ≠ 1` the factor may be applied twice — unless `R̂` is carried in curvature-k units and silently compensates. `zeta=1.0` is the default, so no run to date is affected, and ζ also enters HyperMap's `polar_to_poincare` conversion; the units need tracing end-to-end through the init before calling this a bug either way.

 - Finish the investigation into why HyperMap embeds a balanced tree "nicely" while D-Mercator does not, and whether the Fermi-Dirac latent-geometry model can *generatively recover* a tree at all. Findings so far: D-Mercator's clustering-matching drives β to its floor (β = D + 0.01) because a tree's c̄ = 0 is unreachable, corrupting κ/radii (degree–radius corr −0.25 vs HyperMap's −0.86); forcing a large β does NOT recover the tree (AUC 0.71→0.81, generated clustering ≈ 1.0), and sharpening the threshold on HyperMap's own coordinates *raises* generated clustering (0.24 → 0.40), moving away from the tree's zero. Tentative conclusion: a tree's zero clustering fights the model's quasi-transitivity (proximity ⇒ triangles), so large β cannot recover it; HyperMap merely wins on *layout/edge-ranking* (AUC ~0.99, clean radial hierarchy), not generative fidelity.
